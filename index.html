Section 01 — Greetings

One idea, spoken everywhere

Every language, human or machine, has its own way of saying it. Here are a few of our favorites.

Hola, Mundo

Spanish

Spoken by over half a billion people, and every one of their first programs looked exactly like this.

printf("Hola, Mundo\n");
こんにちは世界

Japanese

Konnichiwa, sekai. Proof that a greeting doesn't need the Latin alphabet to compile on the first try.

fmt.Println("こんにちは世界")
Salut, Monde

French

Somehow even a debug statement sounds elegant. The semicolon, of course, remains non-negotiable.

console.log("Salut, Monde");
Hallo, Welt

German

Efficient, precise, and delivered exactly on schedule — much like the language it's written in.

System.out.println("Hallo, Welt");
Habari, Dunia

Swahili

A greeting shared across East Africa, warm enough to make even a stack trace feel friendly.

puts "Habari, Dunia"
01001000 01101001

Binary

How your computer has been saying hello all along. It just needed someone to listen carefully.

Section 02 — The Story

Where "Hello, World" came from

In 1972, at Bell Labs, Brian Kernighan needed a tiny example program for a tutorial on the B programming language. He chose two words that would outlive the language itself. When they reappeared in The C Programming Language in 1978, a tradition was sealed forever.

Since then, "Hello, World" has been the first program written by nearly every programmer on Earth. It's a handshake between a human and a machine — the moment you learn that the thing on your desk will actually listen to you.

"I have a distinct memory of a cartoon showing an egg and a chick saying 'Hello, world.' It seemed like a natural thing." — Brian Kernighan, on how it all started
